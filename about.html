CorridorBook is a Physical Internet platform. We apply the principles of digital packet routing — openness, interoperability, standardisation — to physical freight in the Rotterdam–Antwerp hinterland corridor.

Why we built CorridorBook

The Rotterdam–Antwerp corridor is one of the busiest freight corridors in the world. Yet barge and rail — the most sustainable and often cheapest modes — run at 40–60% capacity utilisation. The problem isn't infrastructure. It's information.

Shippers default to road because they know exactly what it costs and when it arrives. Barge and rail require phone calls, email quotes, and days of waiting. By the time the answer comes, the truck has already left.

CorridorBook solves this. We connect shippers and operators on a single platform where capacity is visible, prices are live, and bookings take less than 90 seconds. The same way Skyscanner made flying visible — we're doing it for freight.

We are a research-backed project built at the intersection of logistics engineering and Physical Internet theory, originally developed at Breda University of Applied Sciences (BUas) as part of a specialisation in Physical Internet systems.

Foundation
Built on Physical Internet principles
The Physical Internet (PI) is a vision for open, interconnected logistics where freight moves like data packets — efficiently routed through shared infrastructure. CorridorBook implements three core PI principles:
Interoperability
Any operator, any mode, any terminal on one platform. Shippers don't need separate accounts with Contargo, DB Cargo and DSV Road. One search covers all of them.
Standardisation
A common data layer: standardised container types, port codes, pricing components and CO₂ metrics. Operators publish slots in one format. Shippers see one consistent interface.
Real-time visibility
Hidden multimodal bandwidth is the core problem PI research identifies. CorridorBook makes that bandwidth visible — live prices, live capacity, live vessel positions via AIS.
